Line Coding with PSD Comparison

Aim

To analyze and compare various Line Coding Techniques and study their waveform and spectral properties.

  1. To understand categories: Unipolar, Polar, Bipolar, Scrambling, and Partial-Response coding.
  2. To generate the line coded signals for a given binary input sequence.
  3. To plot and compare the input data waveform and the corresponding line coded waveforms.
  4. To compute and analyze the Power Spectral Density (PSD) for each coding scheme.

Theory

Unipolar NRZ
  • Definition: A ‘1’ is +V for full bit duration; a ‘0’ is 0 V.
  • Voltage levels: {0, +V}.
  • Characteristics: Simple; high low-frequency energy; long runs may have no transitions.
  • Advantages: Simplest implementation; low cost; easy detection in short links.
  • Disadvantages: Strong DC; poor clock recovery; unsuitable for AC/transformer coupling.
  • Applications: Short, DC-coupled digital links and basic lab demonstrations.
Unipolar RZ
  • Definition: A ‘1’ is +V for part of bit then returns to 0; a ‘0’ stays at 0.
  • Voltage levels: {0, +V} with return-to-zero within the bit.
  • Characteristics: More transitions than NRZ; improved timing; increased bandwidth.
  • Advantages: Better synchronization than NRZ; reduced sensitivity to timing drift.
  • Disadvantages: Larger bandwidth; DC still present for unbalanced data.
  • Applications: Baseband links where timing recovery is important.
Polar NRZ
  • Definition: Uses +V and −V for bits (level- or transition-based variants).
  • Voltage levels: {−V, +V}.
  • Characteristics: Better power efficiency than unipolar; DC reduced for random/balanced data.
  • Advantages: Good noise margin; reduced DC compared to unipolar; simple decoding.
  • Disadvantages: Long constant patterns can lose sync; baseline wander in some channels.
  • Applications: Baseband digital transmission and data links.
Polar RZ
  • Definition: Each bit returns to 0 within the bit interval with polarity indicating data.
  • Voltage levels: {−V, 0, +V} (within-bit return-to-zero).
  • Characteristics: Strong timing cues; higher bandwidth than polar NRZ.
  • Advantages: Improved synchronization; easier clock extraction.
  • Disadvantages: Higher bandwidth requirement; more complex than NRZ.
  • Applications: Links needing better timing recovery and reduced baseline issues.
Bipolar AMI
  • Definition: ‘1’s alternate +V and −V (marks); ‘0’ is 0 V.
  • Voltage levels: {−V, 0, +V}.
  • Characteristics: DC-free for typical data; sync degrades on long runs of zeros.
  • Advantages: Low DC; transformer-friendly; allows violation-based monitoring.
  • Disadvantages: Long zero strings cause no transitions; may need scrambling.
  • Applications: Telecom baseband systems and legacy line interfaces.
Pseudoternary
  • Definition: ‘0’s alternate +V and −V; ‘1’ is 0 V (inverse of AMI).
  • Voltage levels: {−V, 0, +V}.
  • Characteristics: DC reduced; transition density depends on distribution of zeros.
  • Advantages: Low DC; useful where zero density is high.
  • Disadvantages: Sync issues for long runs of ones (since ‘1’ is 0 V).
  • Applications: Baseband links where symbol polarity assignment is convenient.
Manchester
  • Definition: Mandatory mid-bit transition; direction encodes data.
  • Voltage levels: {−V, +V} with mid-bit transition.
  • Characteristics: Self-clocking; DC null; higher bandwidth due to frequent transitions.
  • Advantages: Excellent synchronization; DC-free; robust clock recovery.
  • Disadvantages: Higher bandwidth (≈2× NRZ); higher transition rate.
  • Applications: LAN/baseband systems (e.g., classic Ethernet) and timing-critical links.
HDB3
  • Definition: AMI + substitution of 4 consecutive zeros using B/V patterns to maintain transitions.
  • Voltage levels: {−V, 0, +V} with deliberate bipolar violations.
  • Characteristics: DC-free; avoids long zero runs; compatible with AMI receivers.
  • Advantages: Good synchronization; low DC; suitable for long-haul links.
  • Disadvantages: Increased encoder/decoder complexity; violation detection required.
  • Applications: E-carrier (E1) PCM transmission systems.
B8ZS
  • Definition: AMI + substitution of 8 consecutive zeros using a violation pattern.
  • Voltage levels: {−V, 0, +V} with specific substitution pulses.
  • Characteristics: DC-free; maintains transition density; prevents sync loss on long zeros.
  • Advantages: Reliable synchronization; AC/transformer friendly; practical for carrier lines.
  • Disadvantages: More complex; substitution detection must be correct.
  • Applications: T1 digital carrier and related North-American telecom links.
Duobinary
  • Definition: Partial-response code using controlled ISI (conceptually y[k] = x[k] + x[k−1]).
  • Voltage levels: Multi-level (typically 3 levels such as −2, 0, +2 or scaled variants).
  • Characteristics: Reduced bandwidth; introduces memory between symbols; needs suitable decoding/precoding.
  • Advantages: Spectrally efficient; suited to band-limited channels.
  • Disadvantages: Receiver complexity; potential error propagation without proper design; sensitive to channel distortion.
  • Applications: High-speed baseband transmission with constrained spectrum (partial-response systems).

Results & Observations

Observations (per scheme)
  • Unipolar NRZ: Strong DC; narrow main lobe; weak sync on long runs; simple but not AC-coupling friendly.
  • Unipolar RZ: Better timing than NRZ; higher bandwidth; DC may still exist for unbalanced data.
  • Polar NRZ: Better power efficiency than unipolar; reduced DC for balanced data; sync issues on long constant patterns.
  • Polar RZ: Regular returns to zero improve timing; higher bandwidth than NRZ.
  • AMI: Near-zero DC; moderate bandwidth; long zero runs reduce transitions without scrambling.
  • Pseudoternary: Low DC; transition density depends on zeros; complements AMI behavior.
  • Manchester: DC-free and self-synchronizing; high bandwidth; robust clock recovery.
  • HDB3 / B8ZS: Maintain synchronization by inserting violations during long zero runs; DC-free and carrier-friendly.
  • Duobinary: Reduced bandwidth via controlled ISI; requires careful receiver/precoding design.
Comparison Summary
  • DC component: Highest in unipolar codes; very low in Manchester, AMI, HDB3, and B8ZS.
  • Bandwidth: RZ and Manchester increase bandwidth; duobinary reduces bandwidth by partial-response signaling.
  • Synchronization: Manchester provides best self-clocking; bipolar schemes need scrambling for long zero runs.
  • Use-cases: Carrier systems prefer DC-free + sync-preserving codes (HDB3/B8ZS); timing-critical links use Manchester.
Conclusion

Line coding is chosen by balancing synchronization, DC content, bandwidth, and complexity. In practice, DC-free and synchronization-friendly schemes (Manchester or bipolar with scrambling such as HDB3/B8ZS) are widely preferred, while partial-response schemes (duobinary) can improve spectral efficiency when controlled ISI is properly handled.
